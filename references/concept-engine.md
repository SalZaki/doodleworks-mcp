# Concept Engine — the distinctive move

How to turn a source into ONE cognitive anchor and reinvent it as a low-tech contraption that Tinku physically operates by hand.

## What this is for

This is the move that makes a Tinku illustration a Tinku illustration. It is not a drawing of the topic. It is a small machine that *enacts* one idea from the topic, with Tinku working it.

The engine adds the house style, the Tinku character, the aspect role line, and the English-only text rule to every image automatically (see `visual-dna.md`). This doc owns the one thing the engine cannot decide for you: **what the picture is of**. Get the anchor and the contraption right here, then write the actual prompt with `prompt-patterns.md`.

Two hard rules sit above everything else:

- **Never illustrate the topic literally.** A page about caching is not a picture of a database. A page about burnout is not a tired person at a laptop.
- **Never a chart, flowchart, infographic, or diagram.** No boxes-and-arrows process map, no pie, no timeline rendered as a ruler. Those are charts wearing a sketch. The output is a *physical contraption*, drawn as one scene.

## Step 1 — pull ONE cognitive anchor

A source — an article, a paragraph, a claim — contains many ideas. You take exactly one. The anchor is the single cognitive thing the picture will enact. There are five kinds. Name which one you are pulling before you draw anything.

### Judgment
A verdict, a trade-off, a "this beats that", a claim that one thing is too much or too little. Anything evaluative.
- *Signals in the source:* "better than", "not worth it", "too early", "overrated", "the real cost is".
- *Becomes:* a weighing, sorting, or measuring rig — Tinku running the apparatus that renders the verdict.

### Process
A sequence of steps, a transformation, an input that becomes an output through stages.
- *Signals:* "first… then…", "the pipeline", "how X is made", "step by step".
- *Becomes:* a line of stations or a hand-fed machine; Tinku pushing material through the stages.

### Structure
How parts relate in space, not time — a hierarchy, a dependency, a layering, what holds up what.
- *Signals:* "built on top of", "depends on", "underneath", "made of three layers".
- *Becomes:* a stack, a scaffold, a rig of supports; Tinku propping, stacking, or bolting parts together.

### State
A condition the system is *in* — stuck, overloaded, leaking, balanced, starved, on fire.
- *Signals:* "jammed", "drowning in", "barely holding", "running hot", "stretched thin".
- *Becomes:* a contraption visibly in that condition; Tinku straining against it or fighting to hold it.

### Metaphor
The source already hands you a figurative image — a "snowball", a "house of cards", a "fork in the road".
- *Signals:* the author's own analogy, an idiom, a comparison.
- *Becomes:* that metaphor built as a literal working device Tinku operates — take the figure of speech at its word.

### Picking when several fit

Most sources offer two or three candidate anchors. Choose the one that (a) carries the page's single idea most directly and (b) most naturally becomes something Tinku can *do* with his hands. If two anchors both fit, the one with a clearer physical action wins — a judgment you can crank beats a structure you can only look at. One anchor per illustration, always. If you find yourself wanting two, that is two illustrations (see `intake.md` for sizing a set).

## Step 2 — reinvent the anchor as a contraption Tinku operates

Take the one anchor and build it as a **low-tech, slightly grotesque but physically plausible** machine, rig, or workshop scene. Then put Tinku inside it, working it by hand.

- **Low-tech.** Levers, cranks, funnels, pulleys, hoppers, pipes, bellows, sticks, string, buckets. Hand-powered, not digital. No screens, no robots, no clean product render.
- **Slightly grotesque, but clean.** It can be ungainly, patched-together, overbuilt, absurd. It is still drawn in clean, even line on white — characterful, not messy; individual, not childish.
- **Physically plausible.** A viewer should believe it could actually move if Tinku turned the handle. Plausible mechanics are what sell the idea; magic does not.
- **Tinku is operating it.** Pulling the lever, turning the crank, feeding the hopper, propping the load, plugging the leak. His expressive face reacts to the effort — straining, squinting, surprised. The contraption embodies the idea; Tinku working it is what makes the idea *happen*.

Translate the abstract claim into the machine's behaviour. "This compounds" is not a label — it is a snowball that visibly grows each loop. "This is the bottleneck" is not a caption — it is a funnel too narrow, with items jammed above it. The mechanism carries the meaning; the few handwritten annotations only name parts (see `prompt-patterns.md` for the Required-text block).

Keep the frame calm. Tinku is drawn small; the contraption and the white space dominate. One Tinku per scene.

## Step 3 — apply the erase-test

This is the single check that separates a real concept-engine illustration from a decorated one.

> **Erase-test:** mentally remove Tinku from the scene. Does the picture still make complete sense on its own?
> - **If yes — it failed.** Tinku was a decoration, a mascot, a figure standing next to a finished diagram. Redraw it.
> - **If no — it passed.** The idea only resolves *because* Tinku is acting on the contraption. His action is load-bearing.

Why it is strict: a decorative Tinku turns the image back into the two things this style refuses — a labelled diagram with a character glued on, or a mascot pose. The whole point is that the *action* carries the concept. If the machine already tells the whole story standing still, you have drawn a chart and parked a character beside it.

**How to fix a failure.** Move the resolving moment into Tinku's hands. Don't draw the snowball already big — draw Tinku cranking the loop that *makes* it grow, mid-turn. Don't draw a finished tower — draw Tinku straining to keep it from toppling with one stick. The difference is between a state that exists and a state Tinku is *causing or fighting*. Put the verb in his hands.

A quick sharper version: if you can describe the picture without using a verb for Tinku, it failed. "Tinku beside a funnel" fails. "Tinku hand-cranking a funnel that is too narrow while items jam above it" passes.

## Worked transformations

Each shows: the **anchor** (and its kind) → the **contraption Tinku operates** → what the **erase-test** checks.

### Bottleneck (state)
- **Anchor:** the system is jammed at one narrow point — a STATE.
- **Contraption:** Tinku hand-cranks a wide hopper that necks down into a funnel far too narrow; items pile up and jam in the throat above the funnel.
- **Erase-test:** remove Tinku and you have a funnel with a pile on top — an ambiguous still life. With Tinku cranking *into* the jam, the strain and the cause are clear. Passes only because he is forcing material through the choke.

### Compounding (process)
- **Anchor:** small gains accumulate and accelerate over repeated cycles — a PROCESS (loop).
- **Contraption:** Tinku turns a crank that rolls a snowball around a little closed track; each loop the ball is visibly bigger than the last.
- **Erase-test:** without Tinku the snowball is just sitting on a track — no sense of *why* it grows. The growth only reads as compounding because Tinku keeps turning the loop. The crank is the verb.

### Technical debt (structure)
- **Anchor:** a fragile system held up by accumulated shortcuts — a STRUCTURE.
- **Contraption:** Tinku strains to hold up a teetering tower of patched, mismatched boxes with a single propped stick, face tight with effort.
- **Erase-test:** erase Tinku and the tower might just be standing — the fragility is gone. The whole idea lives in his strain and the one inadequate stick. Passes because the structure only stays up *because he is holding it*.

### Context-switching (state)
- **Anchor:** constant re-orientation between tasks, with loss on every swap — a STATE.
- **Contraption:** Tinku frantically re-plugs cables into different sockets on a board, dropping things mid-swap, mouth open in alarm.
- **Erase-test:** without Tinku it is a patch panel — a static object, no cost shown. The dropped items and the frantic re-plugging are the loss. Passes because the swap is something he is mid-action on, badly.

### Single source of truth (structure)
- **Anchor:** many outputs all draw from one authoritative origin — a STRUCTURE.
- **Contraption:** Tinku draws from one labelled tank that feeds a row of taps; he works the one tank, and every tap runs from it.
- **Erase-test:** the tank-and-taps alone could read as plumbing. Tinku filling or working the single tank makes "one origin feeds all" the point. Passes because his action is centred on the *one* source.

### Premature optimisation (judgment)
- **Anchor:** effort spent polishing a detail before the whole exists — a JUDGMENT.
- **Contraption:** Tinku lovingly polishes one gear to a high shine while the rest of the machine around it sits unbuilt — bare frame, missing parts.
- **Erase-test:** a shiny gear among unbuilt parts is just an incomplete machine. Tinku *choosing* to polish the one gear is the verdict. Passes because his misplaced effort is the whole judgment.

## Pre-handoff checks

Before you hand a concept to `prompt-patterns.md` to write the prompt:

- [ ] Exactly ONE anchor, and you can name its kind (judgment / process / structure / state / metaphor).
- [ ] The anchor is reinvented as a low-tech, plausible, slightly grotesque contraption — not a literal picture of the topic.
- [ ] It is a physical machine/rig/workshop scene, NOT a chart, flowchart, infographic, or labelled diagram.
- [ ] Tinku is actively operating the contraption with his hands, and his face reacts to the effort.
- [ ] The erase-test passes: remove Tinku and the idea no longer makes sense.
- [ ] You can describe the picture with a verb for Tinku ("cranking", "propping", "re-plugging") — not just "beside".
- [ ] One Tinku, drawn small; the contraption and the whitespace dominate the frame.

See `visual-dna.md` for the locked house style, `composition-patterns.md` for arranging the contraption with a secondary layout archetype, and `prompt-patterns.md` for turning the concept into the page's `prompt` string.
